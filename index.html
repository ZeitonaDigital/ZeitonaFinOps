01 — The Problem

BIM is everywhere.
Cost intelligence is not.

The AEC industry has invested heavily in BIM adoption — but most of that investment stops at coordination and clash detection. When it comes to cost, teams still fall back on spreadsheets and disconnected workflows.

Models built for geometry, not finance

BIM models are structured for design intent. Without proper parameters, naming conventions, and cost classification embedded in the model, quantity extraction becomes unreliable — or impossible.

Teams that model and teams that budget don't talk

Design and estimating departments operate independently. The result: late-stage budget surprises, rework, and scope changes that could have been caught weeks earlier with connected data.

No structured process for BIM-based cost decisions

Most teams don't have a repeatable workflow to go from model to budget. Each project starts from scratch — manual exports, inconsistent quantity lists, and estimates disconnected from the model.

Training that teaches software, not decision-making

Generic BIM courses focus on tools and commands. They don't teach teams how to structure a model for cost, how to validate quantities, or how to use the output in a real project budget.

02 — The Training

Hands-on training built around
your real projects

Not a certification course. Not vendor-sponsored content. A structured, applied program designed to give your team the skills to use BIM as an active cost tool — starting with your next project.

01

Quantity Extraction from BIM

Learn how to configure model parameters, apply classification systems, and extract reliable quantity schedules from Revit and IFC-based models. Move from raw geometry to structured, auditable data.

  • Model parameter setup for cost use
  • Element classification (Omniclass / NBS / custom)
  • Schedule configuration and validation rules
  • Export to cost workflows (Excel, ERP, estimation tools)
02

Cost Estimation Workflows

Build a repeatable bridge between BIM and your cost estimation process. Apply unit pricing, scope structuring, and progressive estimate maturity aligned to project phases.

  • Linking quantities to cost databases
  • Estimate maturity by design stage
  • Scope gap identification from model analysis
  • Review and audit procedures for BIM-based estimates
03

Structuring Models for Budgeting

Understand what a "cost-ready" model looks like and how to get there. Establish internal standards and review checkpoints that make every future model more useful — not just for design, but for financial decisions.

  • Model authoring standards for cost compatibility
  • LOD for cost vs. LOD for coordination
  • Internal BIM templates and naming conventions
  • QC checklists before quantity extraction
Each module is adapted to your team's tools, project types, and existing BIM maturity level.

04 — Outcomes

What changes after training

More accurate cost estimates

Quantities come directly from the model — not manually measured. Fewer assumption gaps, fewer revision cycles, and more confidence in early-stage numbers.

Better decisions in early project phases

When design options can be quickly evaluated for cost impact, teams make faster and more informed go/no-go decisions — before commitments are locked.

Connected design and finance workflows

A shared language between modeling and estimating teams. Instead of siloed handoffs, your project data flows from model to budget with defined checkpoints and accountability.

Reusable internal standards

Templates, checklists, and documented workflows your team owns. The training produces deliverables, not just knowledge — so the process lives on after the sessions end.

Reduced cost overruns

Earlier detection of scope gaps and quantity changes means budget surprises are caught in the model — not on the construction site when correction costs far more.

Faster project handoffs

When BIM data is structured for cost from the start, handoffs between design, estimating, and procurement become faster — because the data is already there and correctly formatted.
